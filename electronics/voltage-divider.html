The humble voltage divider

Two resistors connected in series. It doesn’t get any simpler than that. Put some voltage in and get less voltage out.

The Math

The output voltage of a voltage divider is the voltage on the \(R_2\) resistor.

\[ U_\text{out} = U_{R2} = R_2 \cdot I_2 \]

All components in the circuit are connected in series, therefore \(I_2 = I_\text{in}\). The input current can be calculated with Ohm’s law: \(I_\text{in} = U_\text{in}/(R_1+R_2)\)

\[ U_\text{out} = R_2 \cdot I_2 = R_2 \cdot I_\text{in} = \frac{R_2}{R_1+R_2} \cdot U_\text{in} \]

Let’s explore this equation to gain some understanding what this circuit can do.

The output voltage is the input voltage multiplied by a factor controlled by the two resistances. The numerator is always smaller than the denominator (because of the \(R_1+\ldots\) term), therefore the whole fraction is always less than one. This means the output voltage will always be smaller than the input voltage.

If both resistances are equal (\(R_1=R_2\)) then:

\[ U_\text{out} = \frac{R_2}{R_2+R_2} \cdot U_\text{in} = \frac{U_\text{in}}{2} \]

and the output voltage becomes exactly half of the input voltage.

All these equations are correct only if we do not draw any current from the voltage divider’s output. If we do \(I_2\) is no longer equal to \(I_\text{in}\).

Thévenin’s equivalent

Students typically learn about Thévenin’s equivalent in their first course on electronics and then quickly forget about it. It is, however, a useful tool to simplify electronic circuit analysis. You can bundle a big part of a circuit and see how it interacts with everything else.

Figure: bundle = Thévenin

This simplification helps us to analyse a portion of a circuit in isolation. You can also accurately predict how that subcircuit is going to interact with other parts of the circuit, without caring how connecting circuits are implemented.
